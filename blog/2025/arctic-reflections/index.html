Arctic Reflection(s)

  • 29 November 2025
  • english

I spent this past week in Göteborg for a PhD course on The Arctic in a Changing Climate. There is something about going somewhere else for six days, only to spend the few hours of Swedish November daylight inside a university building.

That being said, the course was great! We were about 25 PhD students from really different disciplines and from day one, we had a super engaged, active classroom environment. I got to use my brain every day to try and understand ongoing research in biology, geology, atmospheric, oceanic, cryospheric science. We had excellent lectures and so much material to look at.

Here are some thoughts I had, they are all over the place and in no particular order. (Note: This is not an attempt to summarize the course!)

It is insane how much we know about how the Arctic responds to a changing climate. Also: It is insane how much we do not know about how the Arctic responds to a changing climate.

All of this is so hard. Accurately mapping vegetation types, recording snowfall, or measuring ocean circulation, none of these are easy tasks. And it's not like model development is easy either!

Arctic science is political, especially when it is observation-based. Political and economical interests affect where field campaigns can happen, which data is accessible to researchers, and in what ways researchers are allowed to collaborate.

It is so cool to see other people trying really hard to understand something. During the lectures and our discussions, it was fun to witness someone switch into research mode. You see it in their eyes.

The Earth system responds to emissions in a gigantic number of ways. I think (well really, I feel) that the changes affecting us as humans are more relevant than others. Talking about the Arctic, this is evident for a lot of the physical mechanisms, for example the feedbacks involving Atlantification and large-scale atmospheric/oceanic circulation. To me it feels less "relevant" or "urgent" to focus on the impacts on Arctic marine ecosystems, since it is not clear that changes there will have direct repercussions for the functioning of our societies.

A contrasting thought: We were talking about shellfish toxins in one of the lectures, and how an increase in them affects marine mammal populations in the Arctic. As humans, we can monitor these toxins and choose not to eat shellfish in response. Other animals do not have this choice. I find this unfair and a good motivator for taking action.

I find it interesting how the scientific community makes choices: When do we consider something to be

  1. ... a problem worth studying?
  2. ... a problem worth prioritizing?
  3. ... a problem worth counteracting?

Definitions matter a lot for scientific understanding and results. Example 1: Whether you compute the AMOC in depth or density coordinates[1]. Example 2: What we consider to be an ice-free Arctic really depends on, e.g., the variable we choose (sea ice extent, sea ice area) and the time scale (first ice-free month? day? summer?). You can end up with really different numbers and this will affect how the message is received by the general public. But don't let that fool you: Arctic sea ice has been disappearing more and more. With a warmer planet, it disappears faster. I am not so sure that the precise numbers matter that much for recognizing the problem and doing something about it.

Training to be a researcher includes training to become really good at identifying and focusing on knowledge gaps. Arguably, we spend much less time practicing to confidently state the gaps we filled. Reminded me of this here from Zack Labe in 2023:

"The point here is that even though many scientists (including myself) are often responding with 'I don't know' (...) we do know quite a bit. It's just that this is very complicated to disentangle so quickly, and there is no simple one-way causal factor to communicate. (...) ('we' are cautious to avoid making sweeping conclusions by nature of training.)"

My favorite paper for the course was this one. I struggled more with the papers on vegetation change, but it got easier when I asked someone who studies this field. I decided to do this based on a suggestion from Gunilla Öberg's book on interdisciplinarity:

"Spend time to understand why things that are boring to you make others excited."

And here are some more thoughts that did not have anything to do with the course:

I got a really nice compliment this week, completely out of nowhere. The joy! We should do this more often.

Cute date idea: Going to a bar and trying (+ failing) to draw things. Remember the shrimp method! I drew "something that feels like home" and a crocodile.

Being able to speak Swedish means being able to see plays in Swedish. This is a way of participating in culture that I missed when I lived in France. It still took me two and a half years to try it here.
