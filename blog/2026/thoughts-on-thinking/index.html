Thoughts on Thinking

  • 03 January 2026
  • english

"... because I cannot think without language."

I made this comment during a rhetorics workshop in early December, and it seemed completely obvious to me. One of the others in the room disagreed immediately, "I do not need language to think," and this marked the beginning of a rabbit hole. I have spent the last couple of weeks thinking a lot about what it's like inside my brain (yikes!) and I even stumbled upon "Tal & Tanke", a Swedish psycholinguistics book from the 1980s filled with essays on thought, language, and the ways in which they are related.[1] I finished the book and, for my own sanity, I will wrap up this endeavor with a short summary of my new thoughts on thinking.

One important distinction I learned about is that between thinking (as in: reasoning, planning, or imagining), and the verbalization of thought. Verbalization is a separate process and, obviously, requires language. Here are two examples from the book to illustrate this:

  1. Speaking three languages does not mean storing all my knowledge in three languages. A given thought I have does not a priori exist in only one language and needs to be translated into the others. Rather it can exist in a "meta language"[2] and I verbalize (translate from) into German, English, or Swedish when expressing it in speech or writing.
  2. Consider the figure below: Can you fold it into a cube? If yes, will the two arrows meet? I do not need language to solve this problem (folding the cube "in my head"). To verbalize how exactly my brain solves this is actually challenging (similar to accurately describing a movie scene). On a related note, I learned that thinking out loud apparently helps with solving text-based problems, while it does not with geometry problems.

Verbalizing a thought affects the thought: It can help with making it concrete and coherent.[3] After formulating it, I think I am more likely to remember the verbalized thought rather than the vague "thought in meta language" from before I said/wrote it for the first time. Now, note that I also remember melodies better when I sing them, and remember visuals better when I draw them myself. Is this the same phenomenon (expression makes thoughts memorable), or something else? I don't know!

I would say that the ideas I reason about the most, or which feel most like I am actively thinking, lend themselves to be expressed in words (or symbols), rather than in melodies or visuals. Similarly, most learning I do happens (at least in part) via language; reasoning about concepts I have acquired through language is hardly possible without language.

The author of the final chapter, Alvar Ellegård, makes the point that thinking provides us with a shortcut for solving problems: Instead of cutting out the figure above and trying to fold the cube, I can emulate the same steps in my head. If the shape is too complex to keep all the moving parts in working memory, thinking is not a shortcut; I'll be better off doing it with my hands.

The power of (human?) brains is that we can imagine more abstract concepts than physical movements or paper cutouts: To add 4+3, I could envision four and three apples and count all of them. But I don't do that anymore, I know the result, and I can work with the concepts (here, numbers) 4 and 3 directly. I don't know the result for 54+23, but I can use the concepts 50, 20, 4, and 3, to derive it.[4] More generally: Language allows us to put labels on increasingly abstract and difficult concepts, and move on to use the labels as shorthands. A larger repertoire of labels enables more complex thought.

So, what would I say in hindsight? I would use a narrower definition of thinking (complex reasoning about abstract concepts), and a wide definition of language (sign language is language; mathematics is language). But yes, I need language for this kind of thinking.
